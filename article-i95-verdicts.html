What a Pennsylvania jury actually awards for an I-95 multi-vehicle crash, 2018 to 2025.

Author Robert E. McCann
Published April 22, 2026
Source PA Unified Judicial System
Dataset 73 verdicts · 7 years
Robert E. McCann Founding Member · Court-Appointed Settlement Master · 25 years on the docket

I pulled the verdict slips on every I-95 corridor multi-vehicle case that reached a Philadelphia County or Delaware County jury between 2018 and 2025. The numbers don't tell the story carriers want you to believe.

Seventy-three cases. Five judges. Two counties. One stretch of road, the section of I-95 from the Walt Whitman Bridge up through Bristol, where most of the multi-vehicle work in Eastern Pennsylvania actually happens.

I started this analysis because a carrier's adjuster told my client that a jury "rarely awards more than three hundred thousand dollars" on an I-95 case. That's the kind of statement that sounds authoritative until you look at the public docket. The Pennsylvania Unified Judicial System publishes verdict slips. They are searchable. They are organized by county, by judge, by case type. Anyone can pull them. Most people don't.

This is what's actually on file.

The dataset

Seventy-three verdicts in Philadelphia County Court of Common Pleas and Delaware County Court of Common Pleas, 2018 through the first quarter of 2025. Filtered to multi-vehicle accidents on I-95 only, excluding single-car loss-of-control claims, excluding pure rear-end fender claims that resolved before discovery. Every case in the set went to verdict. None of them are settlements, none of them are summary-judgment dismissals, none of them are voluntary dismissals.

The reason that filter matters: settlement data isn't public. Carriers know that. The "rarely above three hundred thousand" claim relies on the fact that most cases settle, and most settlement numbers never see daylight. The cases that did go to verdict are the ones a defense team couldn't argue the plaintiff out of. That's a specific population. It's also the only population the public can verify.

Here's what the seventy-three look like, sorted by injury severity and number of defendants.

The dataset median is six hundred eighty thousand dollars. That's the median of the cases that went to verdict on the I-95 corridor in two of the busier PI venues in the country. It is more than twice the number a carrier quoted my client.

That gap, between what carriers cite and what the public docket shows, is the entire reason this article exists.

The verdicts on our ledger aren't a number. They're a record of what the defense couldn't argue against.

What drives the high end

The fourteen highest-verdict cases in the dataset share four characteristics. None of them are an accident.

1. A commercial defendant in the chain

If the at-fault vehicle was operated by a commercial driver, or if a commercial defendant joined the chain, the recovery ceiling moved. Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration regulations apply. Hours-of-service logs become discoverable. Maintenance records become discoverable. Driver qualification files become discoverable. Most carriers don't want any of those things in front of a Philadelphia or Delaware County jury, and a plaintiff who knows to ask for them changes the negotiation posture before the case ever reaches verdict.

2. A multi-defendant fault structure

Pennsylvania uses a fifty-one-percent modified comparative rule. New Jersey uses its own modified rule with a verbal threshold. Delaware applies pure comparative negligence. When more than one defendant is on the hook, joint and several liability changes the math. A plaintiff who is twenty percent at fault still recovers eighty percent. A plaintiff against three defendants whose collective fault adds up is in a different settlement posture than a plaintiff against one.

3. Documented future-medical specials

Every case in the top quartile had a life-care plan filed before trial. Not a treating-physician affidavit. A formal life-care plan with a vocational economist or a certified life-care planner. Future medicals are the largest line item in catastrophic cases, and the absence of a documented future-care number is the single biggest reason cases that should pull seven figures settle for six.

4. A case theory the carrier couldn't reduce to a percentage

Carriers settle to a percentage. Forty-five percent of the medical specials, sixty percent of the wage loss, some multiple of the pain and suffering. The high-verdict cases in this dataset all forced the case theory off that math, into something the adjuster's spreadsheet didn't have a column for. A scaffold-and-truck case that became a refusal-to-load liability theory. A trucking case that became a corporate-failure-to-supervise case. The settlement spread doesn't accommodate cases the carrier hasn't categorized yet.

What this means in practice

The four traits above are what we look for in any I-95 case during intake. A case that has all four moves into trial preparation immediately. A case that has two of four still has room to develop them, but only if work starts inside the first ninety days. A case that has none of them is going to settle to the carrier's spreadsheet, and that's the case where a small firm and a verdict mill firm will produce roughly the same outcome.

What drags the low end

The bottom quartile of the dataset, eighteen verdicts under three hundred thousand dollars, share three patterns.

First, comparative-fault findings above twenty percent. A plaintiff at thirty percent fault recovers seventy percent of the verdict, before reductions. Comparative-fault arguments are the defense's most reliable tool, especially where the plaintiff was on a phone, was changing lanes, or was driving a vehicle the defense can portray as inadequately maintained. The verdict arithmetic isn't the issue. The discovery posture is. Cases that walk into trial without a clear accident-reconstruction posture lose ground on comparative fault, and the verdict reflects it.

Second, missed Pennsylvania PIP coordination. Pennsylvania's tort election structure under 75 Pa. C.S. § 1705 makes the difference between a "limited tort" and "full tort" plaintiff a five-figure to six-figure swing in pain-and-suffering recovery. The lower-verdict cases in this set disproportionately involved limited-tort plaintiffs whose attorney did not take the steps available to recover under one of the limited-tort exceptions. The exceptions exist. They require the right facts and the right pleading.

Third, statute issues. Pennsylvania's PI statute is two years. New Jersey, two. Delaware, two. The discovery rule extends that timeline in narrow circumstances, but only if pled. Cases that came in close to the statutory deadline pled tightly to the standard rule and lost the optionality the discovery rule would have given them. None of the bottom-quartile cases were dismissed on statute grounds. Several of them lost negotiation room because the carrier knew the timeline closed off alternative theories.

What this means before you call any lawyer

Carriers know the public docket. Their reserve numbers reflect it. When a carrier offers a number close to the median above, that's the carrier saying it has read the same data and decided the case is roughly average. When a carrier offers a number well below the median, the carrier is making a bet on what the plaintiff's lawyer doesn't know.

Three things to ask any attorney before you sign a representation agreement on an I-95 case.

  • Will you order independent crash reconstruction within thirty days of intake. The reconstruction work that gets a case into the top quartile of recoveries is the work most plaintiff firms will not pay for unless they have a documented case theory.
  • Will you obtain commercial-driver-qualification files and hours-of-service logs if a commercial vehicle is on the chain. Most carriers won't volunteer these. The discovery requests have to be specific.
  • Will you commission a life-care plan, with a vocational economist, before the demand is filed. Future medicals are the largest verdict line in catastrophic cases. They have to be documented up front.

If the answer to any of those is "we'll see how it develops," you are listening to a settlement firm's pitch. Settlement firms run a different business. Their math depends on volume, not verdict. There's a place for that, but it's not the place a multi-vehicle I-95 case belongs.

What this firm does on these cases

Twenty-five years ago, when I started practicing in Pennsylvania, the I-95 corridor was the fastest-growing PI lane in the state. It still is. We have tried I-95 cases in front of every Common Pleas judge in Philadelphia and Delaware Counties, and we have settled many more before they reached a courtroom. Eight attorneys. Three state bars. Two offices on the corridor. Whatever the I-95 facts are, one of us has stood in the well and argued them.
